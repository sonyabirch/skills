# Screens

Run these on the four or five candidates that survive the cut, not on the long list. Order matters: the cheap screens kill most candidates in minutes, so run them first and only spend real time on what is left.

Report each screen as a fact with its method attached. "Searched TMview for the exact string across EU and DE, one hit in class 42, none in 35" is useful. "Looks available" is worthless and slightly dishonest, because you did not check availability, you checked the first page of a search engine.

---

## 1. Say it aloud

Read each candidate into sentences that will actually be spoken:

- "Hi, this is [name] from ___."
- "___, how can I help?"
- "Sorry, it's ___, spelled ___."
- "You can reach me at hello@___.com."

Count the clarifications. One is survivable, two is a tax you pay forever. Dictating the email address is the harshest and most useful version of this test, because it catches doubled letters, silent letters, and anything where the spelling does not follow the sound in the listener's language.

Also check length in use. Anything over three syllables gets shortened by clients whether you like it or not, so decide now what the short form is and whether you can live with it.

## 2. Search results

Search the bare name on its own, then the name plus the category word. You are looking for who already owns those results. A name whose first page is a bank, a football club or a Spotify artist is a name you will spend years fighting for visibility, and no amount of content will move it. This is a soft kill rather than a hard one, but it is the screen people skip and regret.

## 3. Domain and handles

The exact-match .com is no longer the requirement it was in 2010, so treat it as nice to have rather than as a gate. The real test is whether someone who hears the name can find you by typing it. Check, in order:

- the exact .com
- the sensible national or category alternative (.de, .co.uk, .studio, .agency, .co, .io), preferring one that reads as deliberate rather than as a consolation
- the same handle across the two or three platforms that actually matter for the business

Consistency across handles is worth more than any specific TLD, because inconsistency shows up on every card, signature and profile. Reject hyphens and number substitutions outright: they fail the dictation test in section 1.

If the .com is parked and for sale, note the asking price rather than treating it as unavailable. If it is in live use by a real business in an adjacent field, treat that as a trademark signal and check section 4 carefully.

## 4. Trademark registers

A register search finds identical and near-identical marks in the classes you care about. It does not assess likelihood of confusion, which is the thing that actually decides an opposition, and it does not cover unregistered rights. So this screen can rule a name out with confidence and can never rule one in. Say exactly that when you report it.

Where to look:

- **TMview** (run by EUIPO, aggregates a large number of national and regional offices) is the efficient first stop for anything touching Europe.
- **EUIPO eSearch** for EU trade marks specifically.
- **DPMAregister** for Germany.
- **UKIPO trade mark search** for the UK.
- **USPTO trademark search** for the US.
- **WIPO Global Brand Database** for international registrations.

Search the exact string, then the obvious near-misses: one letter different, singular against plural, with and without a space, and the phonetic equivalents. Identity is not the standard, similarity is.

Classes that matter for a consultancy or agency:

- **35** advertising, business management, business administration. This is the core class for most consulting and marketing work.
- **41** education, training, cultural and event activities.
- **42** scientific and technological services, including software design and development.

A hit in a class you will never operate in is usually survivable. A hit in your core class, by a business the same buyer might plausibly confuse with yours, is a stop.

Escalate honestly. Where the name is about to carry money, a paid identity, a campaign, printed material, say plainly that an hour with a trademark attorney is the only thing that settles this, that it is far cheaper than a rebrand once there is traction, and that the register search you ran is a filter rather than a clearance.

## 5. Company and trading registers

Check whether the name is already in use as a registered company or trading name in the relevant jurisdiction: Handelsregister for Germany, Companies House for the UK, the national equivalent elsewhere. This is separate from the trademark question and catches a different class of collision, namely somebody trading under the name without ever having registered a mark.

## 6. Cross-language

Run this for every language of every market the thing will operate in, and additionally for the major European languages, because accidental collisions do not respect market boundaries.

For each language check four things:

- **Meaning.** Does the string mean something, and is that something unfortunate.
- **Near-homophones.** What does it *sound* like. This catches more problems than exact meaning does, and it is the check people skip.
- **Pronounceability from the spelling.** Can a native speaker say it correctly on first sight, or does stress placement move it somewhere unrecognisable.
- **Inflection.** In German, Russian, Polish and the other case-marked languages, the name will be declined in speech whether you approve or not. Check that it survives an ending, and check the gender it will be assigned.

Search the string as a word in each language rather than relying on a translation tool alone, and where the stakes are high, have a native speaker say it back to you cold with no context.

## 7. Longevity

Three questions, each of which has killed a name that passed everything above:

- Does it lock you into one service line you may drop in three years.
- Does it lock you into one city or country you may leave.
- Does it name a technology or trend that will date the business the moment the trend does.

A name should be able to survive the founder changing what they do, because that is the most likely thing to happen to a small consultancy.

---

## Reporting

Present the screening as a compact block per candidate, with the outcome and the method. Something like:

**Ferro.** Domain: ferro.com in live use by an Italian manufacturer, ferro.studio free. Register: TMview shows two word marks, both class 6 (metals), none in 35 or 42. Company register: no German hit. Language: means "iron" in Italian and Portuguese, no unfortunate reading in DE/EN/FR/RU, one syllable stress, spellable at first hearing in all five. Search: first page is dominated by the manufacturer, which is a real cost. Verdict: clear on the register side, contested on visibility.

Never present a candidate as "available" without naming what was checked and what was not.
